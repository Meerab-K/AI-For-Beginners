**Pac-Man: The Evolution of Game AI**

Since the 1980s, Pac-Man has been a favourite, its ghosts seeming to have personalities of their own. This game has stayed the same for decades, but due to its simplicity and behaviour of its ghosts, it has become common in AI discussions. Pac-Man now serves as a foundation for reinforcement learning research, with AI trained to play as both Pac-Man and the ghosts. This essay breaks down the past, present and future of Pac-Man with AI.

The original 1980 Pac-Man uses fixed, hard-coded rules to give each of its four ghosts unique personalities. Blinky, the red one, focuses on chasing Pac-Man directly and accelerates as the level empties. Pinky, in pink, uses prediction to ambush by using a point ahead of Pac-Man rather than chasing directly. Inky, in cyan, uses a formula that calculates both Pac-Man’s and Blinky’s positions to determine where to go. Clyde, the orange ghost, follows the same timer-based chase-and-retreat pattern as the other ghosts, but additionally alternates between chase and retreat depending on its distance from Pac-Man, making it unpredictable. No learning is involved, just predetermined decisions that are calculated the same way every time. However, these simple rules together create the illusion of coordinated and intelligent teamwork.

In modern times, although the official game itself has not changed, it has become a foundation for reinforcement learning research, where AI agents learn through trial and error rather than following fixed rules. Most of this research trains AI to play as Pac-Man, making decisions for itself based on trial and error. Some research has gone further, replacing the ghosts’ fixed logic with learning-based behaviour, using evolutionary algorithms or adversarial training, where Pac-Man and ghost AIs play against each other and adapt over time. This shows how AI research has shifted from hand-coded rules to building systems capable of learning and discovering strategies on their own.

Looking ahead, one interesting direction would be limiting what AI agents in Pac-Man are allowed to see. Currently, most research agents have access to the full board layout, but a more realistic challenge would involve partial observability, where each agent only perceives what is directly in front of it, relying on other cues, such as sound, to infer the position of nearby agents. An agent bumping into a wall, or the sound of them moving nearby, could act as auditory signals the AI must interpret rather than reading exact positions from a global map. This would push Pac-Man AI closer to challenges faced by real-world autonomous systems, such as robots that must navigate and react using limited, imperfect sensory information rather than a complete view of their environment.

From hard-coded rules in 1980 to AI agents training through trial and error today, Pac-Man is one of many games that has become a way of understanding the evolution of Artificial Intelligence. What started as a simple illusion of personality has grown into research on learning and adaptation, and with ideas like partial observability, Pac-Man will continue to be an influential game for practicing and researching AI.
